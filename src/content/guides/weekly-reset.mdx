## Stop starting the week reactive

A good week rarely happens by accident. It usually starts with twenty quiet minutes before the week begins — a small ritual that turns a pile of open loops into a clear plan.

The Weekly Reset is that ritual on a single page. Four steps, four boxes, twenty minutes.

### The four steps

**1. Look back (5 min).**
Write down last week's wins and lessons. Not a performance review — just "what worked" and "what I'd change". This is where most planning systems skip, and it's the part that actually compounds.

**2. Choose three priorities (5 min).**
Out of everything you *could* do, what are the three things that would make next week a success? If everything is a priority, nothing is. Three is the magic number because it forces a real decision.

**3. Map the week (8 min).**
Put each priority — and the recurring stuff — into specific days. Seeing the week as seven blocks makes overcommitment obvious *before* it happens, not after.

**4. Set the tone (2 min).**
One sentence: how do you want this week to feel? "Calm and focused" leads to different choices than "push hard". Naming it helps.

### A few prompts that make the review honest

- What did I say yes to last week that I should have declined?
- Which priority did I avoid, and what's the smallest first step?
- What's one thing I can *not* do this week to protect the three that matter?

> Twenty minutes of planning routinely saves hours of reactive scrambling. The reset isn't about cramming more in — it's about deciding, on purpose, what gets your attention.
